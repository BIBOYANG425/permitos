## Regulatory Framework

CalARP RMPs are submitted under both California and federal authority. HSC § 25533 establishes that California's accidental release prevention program is based on "the program for prevention of accidental releases of regulated substances adopted by the United States Environmental Protection Agency" under Clean Air Act § 112(r). The CUPA verifies "stationary source registration and submission of an RMP or revised RMP."

---

## Submission System: RMP*eSubmit

The federal EPA requires submission of all RMPs through **RMP*eSubmit**, the EPA's online submission portal. California (via CalARP) piggybacks on this federal submission system — a facility submitting its federal RMP via RMP*eSubmit simultaneously satisfies the federal 40 CFR Part 68 obligation and the CalARP state submission obligation.

**RMP*eSubmit access:** Available through the EPA's Central Data Exchange (CDX) at https://cdx.epa.gov/

**Key RMP*eSubmit steps:**
1. Register at CDX (https://cdx.epa.gov/) with facility credentials.
2. Complete the RMP*eSubmit online form with all required plan elements.
3. Digitally sign and certify the plan.
4. Submit electronically — the system assigns a unique facility ID and confirmation number.
5. The submitted RMP is stored in EPA's RMP database (accessible to regulators, including California CUPAs).

## What Must Be Included in the RMP

An RMP must contain the following major sections (40 CFR Part 68, Subparts A–H, incorporated into CalARP):

### 1. Registration
- Facility name, address, DUNS number
- Owner/operator contact information
- Latitude/longitude
- NAICS code for covered processes
- Program level for each covered process (1, 2, or 3)
- Number of employees
- Emergency contact information (24-hour)

### 2. Hazard Assessment
| Program | Required Scenarios |
|---|---|
| Program 1 | Worst-case release scenario only |
| Program 2 | Worst-case + at least one alternative release scenario |
| Program 3 | Worst-case + at least one alternative release scenario |

- Worst-case scenario: release of the largest vessel or pipe quantity in 10 minutes (toxic) or as a vapor cloud (flammable).
- Alternative release scenario: a more likely release based on facility history or process knowledge.
- Endpoint distances calculated using EPA's RMP*Comp tool or equivalent air dispersion model.

### 3. Five-Year Accident History
All qualifying accidental releases in the prior 5 years (see `five-year-accident-history.md`).

### 4. Prevention Program
| Program | Required Prevention Elements |
|---|---|
| Program 1 | Minimal — coordination with local emergency responders |
| Program 2 | Safety information, hazard review, operating procedures, training, maintenance, incident investigation, compliance audits |
| Program 3 | Full PSM-equivalent elements: process safety information, PHA, operating procedures, training, MOC, pre-startup review, mechanical integrity, incident investigation, compliance audits, employee participation, hot work permits |

### 5. Emergency Response Program
- Emergency response plan (or coordination with local LEPC/fire department for Program 1 facilities).
- Procedures for informing the public and local emergency response agencies.
- Documentation of emergency response equipment available at the facility.
- Training program for employees in emergency response.

## Timing Requirements

| Trigger | Deadline |
|---|---|
| New covered process commences operation | Submit RMP **no later than 3 years after the process becomes subject to regulation**, or before initial startup of the process |
| Routine resubmission | Every **5 years** from the date of the last submission |
| Accidental release requiring offsite response | Revise and resubmit within **6 months** of the release |
| Changes to covered process | Revise if changes affect process safety information; resubmit within required period |
| CUPA or EPA request | As directed by the regulator |

## CUPA Review Process (HSC § 25535)

After submission, the CUPA:
1. **Verifies** that the RMP was submitted (registration check).
2. **Reviews** the RMP for completeness and technical adequacy.
3. **Requests corrections** if the RMP is deficient — the facility must correct and resubmit within the CUPA's specified timeline.
4. **Notifies the facility** of implementation requirements.
5. **Conducts inspections** to verify that the prevention and emergency response programs described in the RMP are actually implemented.

If the CUPA identifies deficiencies during inspection, HSC § 25535 provides a process for compliance schedules and corrective action.

---

## Certification Requirement

The RMP must be **signed and certified** by a senior official with authority to commit the facility to the plan's implementation. The certification attests that:
- The RMP is accurate, complete, and current.
- The prevention and emergency response programs are implemented.
- The facility has complied with all applicable requirements.

---

## California vs. Federal Differences

While CalARP incorporates federal RMP requirements by reference, California adds:
- **CUPA as the local implementing agency** (rather than EPA directly) — inspections are performed by CUPAs.
- **California-specific regulated substances and thresholds** — state threshold quantities may be lower than federal thresholds.
- **SB 740 labor requirement** (effective January 1, 2024): Facilities conducting specified chemical manufacturing or CO₂ work must use skilled, trained workers for construction trades.
- **Land use planning coordination** — CUPAs share RMP information with local jurisdictions for land use decisions under HSC § 25531 et seq.
