## Application Requirements — 40 CFR 70.5

A complete Title V permit application must include all of the following:

### Facility Identification
- Source name, address, and facility identification number (district permit number + EPA ID)
- SIC code(s) and primary NAICS code
- Name, address, and telephone of responsible official
- Name of the district and state agency with Title V authority

### Emission Unit Inventory
- Description of all emission units and associated control devices
- Emission rates in tpy and lb/hr for each regulated pollutant
- PTE calculations supporting the emission rates (see tpy-calculations.md)
- Identification of any emission unit subject to an applicable requirement (NSPS, NESHAP, MACT, RACT, LAER)

### Applicable Requirements
For each emission unit, list every **applicable requirement** — a core Title V concept meaning:
- Any emission standard, work practice, or operating condition established under the CAA (NSPS, MACT, NESHAP)
- Any district rule that implements a CAA requirement (RACT rules, attainment plan measures)
- New Source Review (NSR) permit conditions
- Any state/local requirement that EPA has approved as part of the SIP

**Important:** California district rules that are part of the federally approved State Implementation Plan (SIP) become "applicable requirements" under Title V. This is why a California Title V permit can be 100+ pages — it must compile every SIP rule that touches the source.

### Compliance Certification
- A compliance schedule for any requirement with which the source is not in compliance
- A statement of compliance status for each applicable requirement
- A compliance plan if the source is in violation (see compliance-schedule.md)

### Responsible Official Certification
The application must be signed under penalty of law by a "responsible official" as defined in 40 CFR 70.2 (for a corporation: a president, secretary, treasurer, VP, or a person empowered to bind the corporation). The signature certifies that the information is true, accurate, and complete.

## Permit Content — 40 CFR 70.6

Every Title V permit must contain all of the following elements:

### 1. Emission Limits and Standards
- Numeric emission limits (lb/hr, tpy, lb/MMBtu, ppm) for each pollutant from each unit
- Must include all applicable requirements from federal rules AND the SIP

### 2. Monitoring Requirements
Sufficient to assure compliance with permit terms; must include at minimum:
- **Periodic monitoring**: at a frequency sufficient to yield representative data (minimum once per year for most requirements; some rules require quarterly, monthly, or continuous)
- **Continuous Emission Monitoring Systems (CEMS)**: required when the applicable requirement specifies it (e.g., Acid Rain, Part 75)
- **Parametric monitoring**: Where CEMS is not required, monitoring of operational parameters that correlate to emissions (e.g., pressure drop across a scrubber)

### 3. Recordkeeping
- All monitoring data must be retained for at least **5 years**
- Records of all required inspections, maintenance, calibrations
- Records supporting each deviation report

### 4. Reporting
- **Annual compliance certification** to the permitting authority and EPA Region 9
- **Semiannual (or more frequent) deviation reports** identifying any exceedances of permit terms
- **Prompt reporting** (within 2 business days for emergencies; within 10 days for non-emergency deviations at most districts) [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE — reporting timelines vary by district and deviation type]
- Reports must be submitted electronically in California via the district's online portal (see portal information below)

### 5. General Conditions
- Standard Title V general conditions (emergency provisions, duty to comply, permit shield, reopener clauses)
- **Permit shield**: Compliance with the Title V permit is deemed compliance with all applicable requirements listed in it — this is a key legal protection for operators. It does NOT cover requirements the permit failed to include.

### 6. Federally Enforceable Operational Limits (if applicable)
- Any synthetic-minor-equivalent limits that were the basis for not requiring Title V — once in Title V, these become federally enforceable permit conditions

## Application Completeness — District Review Timeline

Under 40 CFR 70.5(a)(2), the district must determine whether an application is complete within **60 days** of receipt. Until completeness is determined, the clock for permit issuance does not run.

| Milestone | Timeline |
|---|---|
| Initial application receipt | Day 0 |
| District completeness determination | Within 60 days |
| Proposed permit (draft) issued | Typically 12–18 months from completeness (California experience) [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE] |
| Public comment period | 30 days minimum (40 CFR 70.7(h)) |
| EPA Region 9 review | 45 days after proposed permit (40 CFR 70.8(a)) |
| Final permit issuance | After EPA review, no additional objection |

**California-specific:** Districts are required to act on a complete application within **12 months** under HSC §42301.6. If not acted on within 12 months, the applicant may petition ARB. [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE]

## Permit Fees

Title V permit fees in California are set by each air district. Federal law (CAA §502(b)(3)) requires that fees must be sufficient to cover the reasonable costs of the Title V program.

| District | Approximate Annual Title V Fee (2023–2024 figures) |
|---|---|
| South Coast AQMD | $3,000–$25,000+ (based on tpy emissions) [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE] |
| San Joaquin Valley APCD | $1,500–$10,000+ [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE] |
| Bay Area AQMD | $2,500–$15,000+ [VERIFY IN PRIMARY SOURCE] |

Fees are typically based on actual emissions reported in the annual emissions inventory, not PTE.
