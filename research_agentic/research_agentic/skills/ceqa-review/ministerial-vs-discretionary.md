# Ministerial vs. Discretionary Projects Under CEQA

**Sources:**
- California Public Resources Code (PRC) § 21080 — https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=PRC&sectionNum=21080.
- California LCI/OPR CEQA page — https://lci.ca.gov/ceqa/
- Fetched: 2026-06-03

---

## The Core Rule

PRC § 21080(a) establishes that CEQA applies to **discretionary projects proposed to be carried out or approved by public agencies**, including zoning ordinances, variances, conditional use permits, and subdivision maps.

PRC § 21080(b)(1) exempts **ministerial projects** from CEQA. Ministerial projects are the first listed exemption category.

The LCI/OPR CEQA page states: "CEQA requires public agencies to 'look before they leap' and consider the environmental consequences of their **discretionary** actions."

---

## What Makes an Action Ministerial

A ministerial action involves **minimal or no agency discretion** — the agency applies fixed legal standards and has no authority to impose conditions or modify the project based on environmental concerns. Once the applicant meets the statutory or regulatory criteria, approval is mandatory.

**Contrast with discretionary:** A discretionary action requires the agency to exercise judgment — it can approve, deny, or impose conditions based on its evaluation of the project, including environmental factors.

### Statutory Text (PRC § 21080(b)(1))
The statute lists as exempt: "Ministerial projects proposed to be carried out or approved by public agencies."

The statute does not define "ministerial" within § 21080 itself; the distinction is elaborated in the CEQA Guidelines (14 CCR § 15369) and case law.

## Examples by Category

### Ministerial (CEQA does not apply)
| Action | Basis |
|--------|-------|
| Issuance of a building permit for a structure that conforms to zoning and building codes | Fixed-standard approval; no discretion to deny if code is met |
| Business license issuance | Ministerial — applicant meets criteria, license issues |
| Filing and recording a final subdivision map (after discretionary tentative map approval) | Ministerial implementation step |
| Routine vehicle registration | No environmental judgment involved |
| Permits for in-kind replacement of existing equipment | "Replacement in kind" — no new discretion exercised |

### Discretionary (CEQA applies)
| Action | Basis |
|--------|-------|
| General plan amendment | Agency exercises judgment on land use policy |
| Conditional use permit (CUP) | Agency may impose conditions or deny based on findings |
| Variance | Requires findings; agency has discretion to deny |
| Environmental impact report certification | Core discretionary act |
| Rezoning / zoning ordinance adoption | Legislative discretion |
| Tentative subdivision map approval | Agency can impose conditions |
| Hazardous materials facility permit (where conditions may be imposed) | Discretionary if agency can shape approval |

## Key Legal Standard

PRC § 21080(e) defines **substantial evidence** for CEQA purposes as "a fact, a reasonable assumption predicated upon fact, or expert opinion supported by fact." It explicitly excludes speculation, unsubstantiated opinion, and purely social or economic impacts disconnected from physical environmental effects.

This matters for the ministerial/discretionary line: if an agency's approval process requires it to weigh environmental impacts, that is a signal the action is discretionary.

---

## Environmental Review Outcomes

Once a project is confirmed to be discretionary and not otherwise exempt:

- **Negative Declaration (ND):** Adopted when the project "would not have a significant effect on the environment." (PRC § 21080(c))
- **Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND):** Adopted when revisions to the project would avoid significant effects.
- **Environmental Impact Report (EIR):** Required when "there is substantial evidence…that the project may have a significant effect on the environment." (PRC § 21080(d))

---

## Research Guidance

When researching a facility project:
1. Identify the specific approval actions required (permits, entitlements).
2. For each action, determine whether the agency has discretion to impose conditions or deny based on environmental factors.
3. If any approval action is discretionary and not exempt, CEQA review is required for that action.
4. Ministerial permits issued after a discretionary approval (e.g., building permits following a CUP) do not trigger separate CEQA review if the discretionary stage already completed it.
