## The Basic Trigger

An Environmental Impact Report (EIR) is required when there is **substantial evidence in the record** that a discretionary project **may have a significant effect on the environment**.

PRC § 21080(d): An EIR is required when "there is substantial evidence…that the project may have a significant effect on the environment."

The threshold is low — the phrase is "may have," not "will have." If any credible evidence supports a finding of potential significance, an EIR is required rather than a Negative Declaration.

---

## Substantial Evidence Standard

PRC § 21080(e) defines substantial evidence as:
- A **fact**
- A **reasonable assumption predicated upon fact**
- **Expert opinion supported by fact**

Substantial evidence expressly **excludes**:
- Speculation
- Unsubstantiated opinion
- Purely social or economic impacts disconnected from physical environmental effects

## Document Selection Framework

| Determination | Document Required |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Project will not have significant effect on environment | **Negative Declaration (ND)** |
| Project would have significant effect but revisions will avoid it, and there is no substantial evidence it would still have significant effects after revisions | **Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND)** |
| Substantial evidence that project may have significant effect | **Environmental Impact Report (EIR)** |

The "fair argument" standard applies to ND/MND adoption: if a fair argument can be made based on substantial evidence that the project may have a significant effect, the lead agency must prepare an EIR even if there is also evidence it will not.

## Mandatory Findings of Significance (EIR Required)

PRC § 21083 and the CEQA Guidelines (14 CCR § 15065) establish circumstances requiring a mandatory finding of significance — i.e., the agency must find the project has a potentially significant effect on the environment:

### CEQA Guidelines § 15065 Mandatory Findings
A project shall be deemed to have a significant effect on the environment if it:

1. **Substantially degrades environmental quality** — degrades the quality of the environment, curtails the range of the environment, or provides a short-term advantage to the disadvantage of long-term environmental goals.
2. **Substantially impacts sensitive resources** — has impacts which are individually limited but cumulatively considerable.
3. **Causes substantial adverse effects on human beings** directly or indirectly (e.g., health risk from toxic air contaminants, hazardous materials releases).
4. **Conflicts with an adopted environmental plan or goal** of a local agency.

### Specific Significance Thresholds by Resource Area
Lead agencies typically use local thresholds of significance (often adopted by ordinance or CEQA guidelines) and/or statewide significance thresholds. Common trigger areas for industrial/hazardous materials facilities:

| Resource Area | Common Significance Indicator |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Air Quality | Exceeding SCAQMD, BAAQMD, or other air district thresholds for criteria pollutants or TACs |
| Hazardous Materials | Facility siting near schools, residences; risk of upset or accidental release |
| Toxic Air Contaminants | Excess cancer risk > 10 per million (varies by lead agency) |
| Noise | Increases exceeding thresholds at sensitive receptors |
| Traffic/Transportation | VMT increase above baseline (post-SB 743) |
| Biological Resources | Take of listed species; destruction of sensitive habitat |
| Cultural/Tribal Resources | Adverse change to significant historical resource (AB 52 tribal consultation) |
| Greenhouse Gas Emissions | Exceeding lead agency or CARB thresholds |

## Mandatory Findings Required Before EIR Project Approval

When an EIR identifies significant environmental effects that cannot be fully mitigated, PRC § 21081 requires the agency to make **specific written findings** for each significant effect before approving the project:

**Finding Option 1 — Mitigation:** Changes or alterations have been required in or incorporated into the project which avoid or substantially lessen the significant environmental effect.

**Finding Option 2 — Other Agency Jurisdiction:** Such changes or alterations are within the responsibility and jurisdiction of another public agency and not the agency making the finding. Such changes have been adopted by such other agency or can and should be adopted by such other agency.

**Finding Option 3 — Infeasibility:** Specific economic, legal, social, technological, or other considerations, including provision of employment opportunities for highly trained workers, make infeasible the mitigation measures or project alternatives identified in the final EIR.

When Finding Option 3 is used, the agency must additionally find that **specific overriding economic, legal, social, technological, or other benefits of the project outweigh the significant effects** — the Statement of Overriding Considerations (SOC).

> Statutory authority: PRC § 21081, amended effective October 1, 1994.

## Lead Agency Responsibilities for EIR Preparation

PRC § 21082.1 requires the lead agency to:
1. **Independently review and analyze** any report or declaration required by CEQA.
2. **Circulate draft documents** that reflect its independent judgment.
3. Make a **certification finding** at approval that materials reflect the independent judgment of the lead agency.
4. **Submit environmental documents electronically** to the State Clearinghouse (LCI).
5. **Post all environmental review documents** on its website.

Environmental documents must be prepared directly by, or under contract to, a public agency — not solely by the applicant.

## Programmatic vs. Project-Level EIRs

For industrial facilities, EIR coverage may occur at multiple tiers:
- **Programmatic EIR** — covers a broad program (e.g., general plan, area plan); later projects may tier off it.
- **Project EIR** — focuses on a specific proposed project.
- **Subsequent/Supplemental EIR** — required when a previously approved project undergoes substantial changes, or when new significant information arises.

---

## Research Notes for EHS Facilities

Hazardous materials facilities frequently trigger EIR review under these pathways:
- Risk of accidental release posing threat to nearby sensitive receptors (schools, residences, hospitals).
- Air quality impacts from process emissions (criteria pollutants or TACs) exceeding local thresholds.
- Hazardous waste generation or storage changes requiring discretionary permit amendments.
- Expansion of operations requiring new or modified CUP or conditional approvals.

Always check the local agency's CEQA thresholds of significance — they often set numeric triggers (e.g., lb/day of criteria pollutants, acreage of habitat disturbed) that translate directly into EIR-required findings.
