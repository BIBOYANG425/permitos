## Why Process Classification Matters

The ROC content limit that applies to an ink and the fountain solution limit (if any) both depend on which printing process is in use. The exemptions also turn on process type (e.g., digital printing is entirely excluded). Classify the process before applying any threshold.

## Printing Process Definitions (Rule 74.19 §G)

### Lithographic Printing (§G.28)
Printing by a planographic method in which the image and non-image areas are on the same plane and are chemically differentiated. The standard commercial printing method for magazines, newspapers, commercial print.

- **Offset Lithographic Printing (§G.34):** Image is offset from a plate to a rubber blanket and then to the substrate. The most common commercial variant.
- **Heatset Ink (§G.23):** Used on continuous web-feed presses equipped with dryers or ovens. The ink dries/sets by heat-induced evaporation of ink oils and subsequent chilling by chill rolls. Heatset presses trigger the most stringent fountain solution limits.
- **Nonheatset Ink (§G.32):** Sets and dries by absorption into the substrate and hardens by ambient air oxidation (may be accelerated by IR, UV, or electron-beam radiation but without heat from dryers or ovens). Sheet-fed offset lithographic presses typically use nonheatset inks.

**Fountain solution applicability:** Fountain solution limits under §B.2 apply to all lithographic printing operations. See voc-content-limits.md for the limit table by press type.

### Flexographic Printing (§G.14)
A printing method using a raised image carrier on a flexible printing roll to apply words, designs, or pictures onto a substrate. The roll is typically rubber or other elastomeric material. Ink is metered via an anilox roll (steel or aluminum core coated with industrial ceramic containing millions of fine dimples/cells).

**ROC limit under §B.1:**
- Flexographic inks on **porous substrates**: 225 g/L LW&EOC (1.88 lb/gal) — the most stringent ink limit in §B.1.
- Flexographic inks on **nonporous substrates**: the general ink limit of 300 g/L LW&EOC (2.5 lb/gal) applies [VERIFY — §B.1 specifies the 225 g/L limit only for porous substrates; confirm no separate nonporous flexo limit in current rule text].

**Specialty Flexographic Printing (§G.59):** Flexographic printing on polyethylene or polypropylene food packaging, fertilizer bags, or liquid-tight food containers. This distinction matters for cleaning solvent limits (see cleaning-solvents.md).

### Gravure Printing (§G.21)
An intaglio printing method in which ink is transferred from minute etched wells on a plate to the substrate, supported by an impression roller, with excess ink removed by a doctor blade. Higher ink volumes than lithographic; typically uses solvent-borne inks with high ROC content.

- **Publication Gravure (§G.45):** Gravure printing on paper subsequently formed into books, magazines, catalogues, brochures, directories, newspaper supplements, or other types of printed material.
- **Packaging Gravure (§G.37):** Gravure printing on paper, paperboard, foil, film, or other substrates to produce containers or packages.

**Add-on controls:** Publication gravure requires higher combined capture/control efficiency (85%) vs. other printing types (80%) when using emission control as an alternative to ink limits (see add-on-controls.md, §B.6.a).

### Letterpress Printing (§G.27)
A printing method in which the image area is raised relative to the nonimage area and the ink is transferred directly from the image surface to paper.

### Ink-Jet Printing (§G.25) — Digital Printing
Printing methods where liquid ink is transferred at high velocity through a small diameter opening to a solid substrate. Ink jet systems include: air-brush, thermal-jet, drop-on-demand using piezoelectric crystals, and continuous with ink recycling.

**Important:** "Digital Printing Operations" (§G.12) — operations using a digital printer that uses digital data to control the deposition of ink, toner, or dye — including inkjet, thermography, electrophotography, and magnetography — are **excluded from Rule 74.19 entirely** under §C.2.f. Digital printing is not subject to Rule 74.19 ink limits or cleaning solvent requirements.

### Screen Printing (§G.55)
A process where printing ink passes through a web or fabric to which a refined form of stencil has been applied. The stencil openings determine the form and dimensions of the imprint.

**Important:** Screen printing is **excluded from Rule 74.19** under §C.2.a. Screen printing is separately regulated under **Rule 74.19.1**.

## Ink Type Definitions

### Process Ink (§G.43)
The hues yellow, magenta, and cyan, plus black, used in the four-color print process (CMYK).

### Pantone Color (§G.38)
A printing ink created for color matching by combination of process inks. Pantone colors are often separately tracked for emission calculation purposes (see recordkeeping.md, §D.4.a).

### Heatset Ink (§G.23)
Used on continuous web-feed printing presses equipped with dryers or ovens. Dries by heat-induced evaporation of ink oils and chilling by chill rolls.

### Nonheatset Ink (§G.32)
Sets and dries by absorption into the substrate and hardens by ambient air oxidation (possibly accelerated by IR, UV, or electron-beam radiation, but without heat from dryers or ovens).

### Radiation Curing (§G.46)
The use of electronic radiation (Electron Beam or ultraviolet light) to polymerize or cure specially formulated inks, coatings, or adhesives. UV-cured inks require a different test method (ASTM Method D5403-93(2007)) because the ROC weight percent is determined, requiring the ink density.

### Sterilization Indicating Inks (§G.60)
Inks that change color to indicate that sterilization has occurred (used for medical instruments, autoclave efficiency monitoring, thermal processing of foods). These inks are **excluded from Rule 74.19** under §C.2.e.

## Substrate Classification

Substrate type controls which flexographic ink limit applies and the fountain solution limit for sheet-fed lithographic presses.

| Substrate Type | Definition (§G.33, §G.39) | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Porous | Any surface permeable to water | Paper, cardboard, paperboard, any paper product coated with a porous material |
| Nonporous | Any surface whose surface prevents penetration by water | Foil, polyethylene, polypropylene, cellophane, metalized polyester, nylon, mylar; paper-like substrates coated with a nonporous material |

## Adhesives and Coatings in Graphic Arts Context

### Adhesive (§G.1)
A material applied for the primary purpose of bonding two surfaces together by surface attachment. In graphic arts: adhesives used in binding or laminating of magazines, books, or other printed materials. ROC limit: 150 g/L LW&EOC (1.25 lb/gal) under §B.1.

### Coating (§G.10)
A layer of material applied to a substrate in a relatively unbroken film to perform a protective or decorative function. ROC limit: 300 g/L LW&EOC (2.5 lb/gal) under §B.1.

### Lamination (§G.26)
A process of bonding two or more layers of material to form a single, multiple-layer sheet by using adhesive.

## Process Classification Decision Tree

```
What is the printing technology?
├── Digital (inkjet, electrophotography, thermography, magnetography)
│   └── EXCLUDED from Rule 74.19 (§C.2.f)
├── Screen printing
│   └── EXCLUDED from Rule 74.19 → go to Rule 74.19.1 (§C.2.a)
├── Proof press only
│   └── EXCLUDED from Rule 74.19 (§C.2.g)
├── Flexographic
│   ├── Substrate = porous → Ink limit 225 g/L (§B.1)
│   └── Substrate = nonporous → Ink limit 300 g/L (§B.1) [VERIFY]
├── Gravure
│   ├── Publication gravure → Ink limit 300 g/L; add-on control 85% efficiency
│   └── Packaging gravure → Ink limit 300 g/L; add-on control 80% efficiency
├── Letterpress → Ink limit 300 g/L (§B.1)
└── Lithographic (offset)
    ├── Heatset web-fed → Ink limit 300 g/L; fountain solution limits §B.2.a
    ├── Non-heatset web-fed → Ink limit 300 g/L; fountain solution limit 50 g/L; alcohol prohibited
    └── Sheet-fed
        ├── Sheet > 11×17 in OR reservoir > 1 gal → fountain solution limits §B.2.c
        └── Sheet ≤ 11×17 in AND reservoir ≤ 1 gal → fountain solution limits §B.2.d
```
